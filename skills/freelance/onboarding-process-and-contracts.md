# Web Design Onboarding Process

Personal reference guide. Source: Self-Made Web Designer (YouTube). Video 3 in the project workflow series (after Sales Call and Proposals).

**Core principle:** Most project problems (missed deadlines, endless revisions) can be solved *before the project starts* with a solid onboarding system.

---

## The Two Things That Delay Every Project

1. **You don't get what you need from clients** (copy, images, assets).
2. **The revision process spirals** into an endless loop.

Both are fixed during onboarding, not during the project.

---

## Deadlines with Consequences

Don't just ask nicely and hope things show up. Set clear deadlines with real fees attached.

- **Late content:** $150 late fee.
- **Ghosted for a month:** Project goes on hold + $500 restart fee.

Explain this before the project starts. Clients actually thank you for helping them stay on track.

## Revision Policy

Define this upfront so there's no ambiguity:

- **Two rounds of revisions total.** Round 1 = homepage. Round 2 = all other pages.
- **48-hour feedback window.** If you don't hear back in 48 hours, it's automatic approval and the project keeps moving.
- If something's unclear, send a quick follow-up or hop on a Zoom to clarify in real time.
- No pixel-nudging back and forth. The structure prevents it.

## Four Phases of Onboarding

### Phase 1: Setup

Starts the moment the client signs the contract and sends first payment.

**Google Drive = single source of truth.** Train the client to look there first before coming to you.

What's in the Drive:
- Quick links to the project
- Help docs for the client
- A folder for every page, each containing:
  - Google Doc for that page's copy
  - Media folder for images/assets
  - Google Sheet for links and embeds
- Site map
- Contract
- Everything else discussed

**Key systems:**
- Pre-written email templates — tweak with client info, don't rewrite from scratch each time.
- Use the Chrome extension **"Copy Folder"** to duplicate your template folder structure instantly (small cost, saves hours monthly).
- Every issue that's ever slowed you down → create a template or doc to prevent it next time.

### Phase 2: Questionnaire

Send a comprehensive intake form. Warn the client it's big, but it's worth it.

Question categories:
- **Core motivation:** "What's at stake if nothing changes on your website?"
- **Positioning:** "What makes you different?"
- **Audience insight:** "What does your audience really care about most?"
- **Brand preferences:** "What other sites really inspire you?"

Go deep — not just surface-level stuff.

### Phase 3: Research

Take questionnaire answers and bridge the gaps.

**Research workflow:**
1. Review their answers thoroughly.
2. Analyze competitors and look for design patterns in their industry.
3. Use ChatGPT to summarize industry trends, audience behaviors, and messaging ideas based on their niche.

**Mood board + Style scape workflow (in Figma):**
1. Hunt for inspiration — real sites that match what the client described.
2. Screenshot sections, link back to original sites for reference.
3. Look for ideas around layout, typography, color, and imagery.
4. Test font combinations and color palettes (start with direct pulls from inspiration sites, refine from there).
5. Assemble a **style scape** — a single mockup layout with fonts, images, colors, and placements. Not a full design, not a wireframe. Just enough for the client to react to.

> Style scape concept from Chris Do. Most clients can't tell you what they like without seeing something. Laying out color + type + images helps things click.

Style scapes save you from designing an entire site only to hear "this isn't what I was envisioning."

### Phase 4: Kickoff Call

Final step before any design work begins. Serves two purposes:

**1. Content deadline.** This is when the client must have everything ready — copy, images, assets. It's a live Zoom call, so showing up unprepared gets awkward fast. That pressure motivates them.

**2. Alignment check.** Walk the client through your style scape and mood board. Share initial design direction and ideas. Ask clarifying questions. Make sure you're in sync before a single page gets designed.

## The Full Flow

```
CLIENT SIGNS CONTRACT + FIRST PAYMENT
        ↓
PHASE 1: SETUP
  → Duplicate Google Drive template (Copy Folder extension)
  → Send pre-written welcome emails (tweaked per client)
  → Share Drive as single source of truth
        ↓
PHASE 2: QUESTIONNAIRE
  → Send comprehensive intake form
  → Covers motivation, positioning, audience, brand preferences
        ↓
PHASE 3: RESEARCH
  → Review answers, analyze competitors
  → ChatGPT for trends/messaging ideas
  → Build mood board + style scape in Figma
        ↓
PHASE 4: KICKOFF CALL
  → Client brings all content (copy, images, assets)
  → Walk through style scape + mood board
  → Align on direction before designing
        ↓
DESIGN BEGINS
```

---

## Key Policies to Set During Onboarding

| Policy | Details |
|---|---|
| Late content fee | $150 |
| Ghost/restart fee | $500 (after ~1 month inactive) |
| Revision rounds | 2 total (1 for homepage, 1 for all other pages) |
| Feedback window | 48 hours per round, then auto-approved |
| Content deadline | Everything due by kickoff call |
| Single source of truth | Google Drive — train client to check there first |
